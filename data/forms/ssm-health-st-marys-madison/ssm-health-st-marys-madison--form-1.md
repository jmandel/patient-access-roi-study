## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Patient access vs. third-party release:** This form is a **general-purpose authorization** form, not specifically designed for patient self-access. Section 3 instructs: "(If release is to self, state self)" ‚Äî so a patient requesting their own records would write "self" in the recipient field. By contrast, form-2 explicitly includes a "For personal access" purpose option with sub-choices for "Copy," "Inspection," and "Summary."

**Purpose options (Section 4):** Includes "Personal Use" as one checkbox option among others (Continuing Care, Transferring Care, Insurance, Disability, Legal, Other). This at least acknowledges patient self-requests.

This references inspection and copy rights but does **not** cite HIPAA ¬ß 164.524 or use the phrase "Right of Access" explicitly.

**Fees:** The back page states: "SSM Health may charge a reasonable fee for viewing, copying, postage and preparation of records to fulfill this request. ## 6. COMPLIANCE

**Expiration (Section 7):** "This authorization will expire on ___. If I do not indicate a date, this will expire one (1) year from the date of my signature below." A one-year default expiration is standard and not unreasonable for a general authorization.

**Submission methods:** The form does not explicitly state how to submit it, though the back page provides mailing addresses and fax numbers for specific facilities. There is no explicit prohibition on electronic submission, but no electronic submission pathway is offered either. The "A photocopy of this authorization is as valid as the original" statement is helpful.

**No notarization required.** No in-person-only submission requirement. No identity verification beyond signature.

**Minor representative provisions:** Section 8 requires a representative to provide name and relationship but the form does not demand notarized proof of authority on its face (the back page does state that representatives "must state their relationship to the patient and provide proof of legal authority").

**Fees for "viewing":** The back page mentions charging for "viewing" records. Under HIPAA, a covered entity may not charge for supervised inspection of records by the patient, though they may charge for copies. The language "a reasonable fee for viewing, copying, postage and preparation of records" could be read as charging for inspection, which would be problematic under 45 CFR ¬ß 164.524(c)(4).
