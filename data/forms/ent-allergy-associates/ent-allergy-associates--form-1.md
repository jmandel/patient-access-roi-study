**Length and structure:** The form is 2 pages with 13 numbered items, plus a footer note on HIV-related information. The length is proportionate to its content, though the dense paragraph format makes it feel longer than necessary.

**Language:** The form is written in moderately dense legal language. The opening paragraph ‚Äî "I, or my authorized representative, request that health information regarding my care and treatment be released as set forth on this form. In accordance with New York and/or New Jersey State Law and the Privacy Rule of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA), I understand that:" ‚Äî is a long run-on sentence that sets a legalistic tone. The numbered items that follow are mostly comprehensible but include constructions like "except to the extent that action has already been taken based on this authorization" that could challenge low-literacy readers.

The "Reason for release" section offers only two options: "At request of individual" and "Other."

**Information density:** Items 1-5 are boilerplate HIPAA/state-law disclosures. Items 6-13 are the operational portions. The ratio of disclosure text to actionable content is high, which may overwhelm patients.

## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Patient access vs. third-party release:** This form is primarily designed as a third-party release authorization, not a patient-access request. The structure assumes records will be sent to a named recipient (Item 8: "Name and address of person(s) or category of person to whom this information will be sent"). A patient requesting their own records must list themselves as the recipient, which is not intuitive. There is no dedicated patient-access pathway or language referencing the right to obtain one's own records.

**Scope options:** The form offers three scope choices: records within a date range, the entire medical record (with a detailed description: "patient histories, office notes (except psychotherapy notes), test results, radiology studies, films, referrals, consults, billing records, insurance records, and records sent to you by other health care providers"), or an open-ended "Other" field. This is reasonably flexible.

**Delivery format:** No delivery format options are offered. There is no mention of electronic delivery, email, patient portal download, or any format preference. The form implicitly assumes paper/physical delivery.

**Special categories:** The form includes separate initialing lines for authorization to release Alcohol/Drug Treatment records, Mental Health Information, and HIV-Related Information ‚Äî properly handling these sensitive categories under NY and NJ state law.

**Right of Access language:** The form does not reference HIPAA ¬ß 164.524 (the Right of Access provision) or explain patient rights to obtain their own records. Item 4 states "My treatment, payment, enrollment in a health plan, or eligibility for benefits will not be conditioned upon my authorization of this disclosure," which addresses anti-coercion but not access rights.

**SSN collection:** The form asks for the patient's Social Security Number in the header fields. This is an unnecessary data collection point for a records release request and raises privacy concerns.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

- **No fee disclosure:** The form does not mention any fees for records copies. While this avoids the problem of discouraging requests, it also means patients have no upfront information about potential costs.
- **Expiration:** Item 11 asks for a "Date or event on which this authorization will expire" ‚Äî this is appropriate and gives the patient control. However, there is no default expiration if the patient leaves this blank, which could result in an indefinitely valid authorization.
- **No unreasonable procedural barriers:** The form does not require notarization, witnesses, or in-person submission. However, the lack of any submission instructions means patients have no guidance on how or where to return the completed form.
- **Anti-redisclosure notice:** Item 2 properly includes the required notice about redisclosure restrictions for HIV, substance abuse, and mental health information, including contact information for the NY Division of Human Rights, NYC Commission on Human Rights, and NJ Division on Civil Rights.

This is an unusual and potentially confusing restriction ‚Äî it implies the authorization does not permit general verbal discussion of the patient's care with the recipient, only written release, unless the recipient is an attorney or government agency.
