## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Patient access vs. third-party release**: This is a general authorization form for disclosure, not a patient-access-specific form. The form says *"I request my PHI be released to:"* followed by a blank for "Name of Person or Institution" ‚Äî it does not distinguish between a patient requesting their own records and a patient authorizing release to a third party. The notes indicate Penn Medicine directs patients wanting their own records to the myPennMedicine patient portal instead.

**Scope options**: Good. Patients can select specific Penn Medicine locations, specific record types (15 options), date ranges, and can opt in/out of sensitive records (HIV/AIDS, psychiatric, substance use). The purpose field offers checkboxes for Legal, Insurance, Personal, and Continuation of Care.

**Delivery method options**: The form offers three delivery methods: US Mail (Paper), CD, and Email (with a note that it is "not secure" and file size limits apply). This is better than many forms that offer only mail, though it lacks a secure electronic delivery option.

**Security warnings**: The form includes a detailed warning about electronic delivery risks: *"I understand that the CD/disc of images are not encrypted and may be accessible to others if the CD/disc is lost or stolen. I also understand that unencrypted email is not secure ‚Äî and therefore may be intercepted by others."* While transparency about risks is appropriate, the emphasis on insecurity without offering a secure alternative may discourage patients from requesting electronic delivery.

**Right of Access language**: The form does not explicitly reference HIPAA ¬ß 164.524 or the patient's right of access. The instructions on page 2 do reference federal law regarding denials and the right to have a denial reviewed, but do not cite specific regulations.

**EHI Export**: No mention of electronic health information export, bulk data, or TEFCA.

**Processing time**: Page 2 states Penn Medicine will comply within 30 days for on-site records and 60 days for off-site records, with a possible 30-day extension ‚Äî consistent with HIPAA requirements.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

**Authorization expiration**: 180 days from signature. This is a reasonable timeframe ‚Äî not so short as to be burdensome, not so long as to create privacy risks.

**Revocation**: Clearly stated that revocation must be in writing and does not apply retroactively.

**Treatment not conditioned on signing**: The form explicitly states *"My refusal to sign this authorization will not affect my ability to receive treatment."*

**Fee disclosure**: Page 2 states *"Penn Medicine will charge for copying records in accordance with Pennsylvania, New Jersey and Delaware law, as applicable."* It adds that *"Patient Cost for Radiology images and reports will be free of charge."* No specific fee amounts are disclosed, and the reference is to state law rather than HIPAA's cost-based fee limitation for patient access requests. This framing could result in patients being charged more than HIPAA allows if the request is for their own records (patient access) rather than third-party disclosure.

**Fax restriction**: *"Penn Medicine will not send medical information by facsimile unless the information is needed for patient care and delay in the transmission of the information would compromise patient care."* This is a restriction on an otherwise-standard delivery method.

**Denial and appeal**: The form references the right to have a denial reviewed by a licensed health care professional, with contact information for the Penn Medicine Chief Privacy Officer.
