## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Dual-purpose form:** The form serves both patient access (Right of Access under HIPAA) and third-party release purposes. It does not distinguish clearly between these two very different use cases. A patient simply wanting their own records must navigate the same form as someone authorizing disclosure to an insurer or attorney. The "Release From" and "Release To" fields assume a sender/receiver model that may not intuitively map to "I want my own records."

**Fee language:** The form states: "I may review my medical record without a charge. If I request copies of my medical record, I may receive the first copy without a charge, additional copies may be charged." Offering the first copy free and free review access is patient-friendly and consistent with HIPAA's requirement for reasonable cost-based fees.

**Photo ID requirement:** The form requires: "I will submit a copy of a picture ID if requesting information by fax, mail, or email" and "I will provide a picture ID prior to accessing my medical record." Identity verification is reasonable, but requiring a photo ID copy for every request method could be a barrier for patients who lack government-issued photo ID.

**Supervised review:** The statement "A HIM professional will supervise the review of my medical record" implies that in-person review of one's own records requires staff supervision, which may feel intrusive or paternalistic.

**Patient portal option:** Including patient portal access as a delivery option, with a simple instruction ("You will be sent a link to get signed up"), is a forward-looking approach that acknowledges digital health infrastructure.

**Email option with informed consent:** The form offers unencrypted email delivery with a clear risk statement: "Health Information sent via unencrypted email may place risk of inappropriate access to the information contained within the email." It requires patient initials to accept this risk. This respects patient autonomy while ensuring informed consent.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

**Authorization expiry:** The form defaults to a 1-year expiry, with an option for the patient to request a shorter period. This is reasonable and common.

**Revocation rights:** Clearly stated ‚Äî "I may revoke this authorization in writing at any time, except to the extent that action has already been taken to comply with it."

**Treatment non-conditioning:** The form explicitly states: "Fallon Medical Complex will not condition treatment on whether the individual sign the authorization." This satisfies the HIPAA requirement.

**42 CFR Part 2 reference:** The form references protections for substance use disorder records under 42 CFR Part 2, though the sentence contains a typographical error: "unless the disclosed includes records from a federally-assisted programs specifically **proving** diagnosis" (should be "**providing**").

**Redisclosure warning:** Present and adequately stated.

**Right to inspect:** References ¬ß164.524 of the Privacy Act, which is the correct HIPAA provision for patient access rights.

**Witness signature:** The form includes a "Witnessed Signature" field in the "For Office Use Only" section. This is for internal use and does not appear to be required of the patient.

**Authorized representative:** The form appropriately requires documentation of authority for representatives other than parents of minor children (e.g., Health Care Power of Attorney).
