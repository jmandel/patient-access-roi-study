## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Patient access vs. third-party release**: This form is structured as a **release/disclosure** authorization ‚Äî the patient authorizes a provider to send records TO the WWMG GI Department. The "disclose this health care information to" section is pre-filled with the GI department's address. This is not a patient access request form; it's designed for transferring records between providers. A patient requesting their own records would likely need to use form-1 instead, which explicitly includes "Personal (to patient)" as a purpose option.

**Scope options**: The form offers four scope choices:
1. "All health care information in my medical record"
2. Information "relating to the following treatment or condition" (with a write-in line)
3. Information "for the date(s)" (with a write-in field)
4. "Other (e.g., X-rays, bills), specify date(s)"

This is a reasonable set of options.

**Purpose options**: The form lists:
- "At my request"
- "Check only if for marketing purposes"
- "Check only if WWMG will be paid or get something of value for providing health information for marketing purposes"
- "Other (specify)"

The marketing-related checkboxes are unusual on a GI department transfer form and appear to be boilerplate HIPAA language rather than practically relevant options.

**Format options**: No electronic delivery options are mentioned. No email, patient portal, or digital transfer options.

**Right of Access language**: The form includes detailed HIPAA rights language on page 2, including the right to revoke authorization in writing, the right to refuse to sign without affecting treatment eligibility, and a re-disclosure warning. However, it does not cite specific HIPAA sections (e.g., 45 CFR ¬ß 164.524).

**EHI Export**: No mention of electronic health information export or bulk data access.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

**Expiration**: The form states: "This document does not permit disclosure of health information created more than 90 days after the date it is signed." The default expiration is "In 90 days for the date signed," with an option to specify a different date or event, but all are capped: "(No longer than 90 days from date signed)." A 90-day hard cap on authorization validity is restrictive. While HIPAA does not prescribe a specific expiration period, an unreasonably short window could require patients to repeatedly re-authorize ongoing transfers (e.g., for continuing care coordination).

**Fees**: The form includes a note: "***Note: there may be a charge for copying medical records, this service provided by an outside agency***". This discloses potential fees but provides no specifics about amounts, which creates uncertainty for patients.

**Revocation**: The form clearly states that revocation must be done "in writing by sending a letter to the person or organization listed above." This is acceptable under HIPAA.

**Treatment conditioning**: The form includes appropriate language that signing is not required for treatment, payment, or enrollment, with a narrow exception for research-related treatment or services "provided solely for the purpose of providing health information to someone else."

**Submission method**: The form provides no guidance on how to submit it (fax, mail, in-person, email). Unlike form-1, which directs patients to "wwmedgroup.com/medical-records-request," this form offers no submission instructions.
