## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**This form is specifically designed for patient access to their own records**, which is exactly what HIPAA's Right of Access (45 CFR ¬ß 164.524) envisions. The title "Patient Request for Access to Designated Record Set" uses the correct HIPAA terminology ("designated record set"). The form's existence as a distinct document separate from the general third-party authorization form (form-1) demonstrates that Providence understands the difference between patient access and third-party release.

**Scope options**: Patients can select specific record types, date ranges, and specific facilities/providers. The "Last 2 years only" checkbox is a practical convenience option.

**Delivery format**: Five options (MyChart, Email, Paper, Disc, Fax) represent strong electronic delivery support. MyChart integration is particularly noteworthy ‚Äî it meets patients where they likely already have an account.

**Recipient flexibility**: The form defaults to "Myself at the address above" but also allows specifying "the following recipient," accommodating patients who want records sent to a new provider or other party. This dual-purpose design is practical.

**What's missing**: The form does not explicitly reference HIPAA ¬ß 164.524 or patient rights language. There is no statement informing patients of their right to receive records, the required response timeline (30 days), or their right to appeal a denial. There is no mention of electronic health information (EHI) export or bulk data access under the 21st Century Cures Act / ONC information blocking rules.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

**Fees**: The form states "Fees may be associated with this request" but does not specify amounts or reference the HIPAA-permitted fee structure (cost-based fees for patient access requests). The vagueness is neither unusual nor ideal ‚Äî patients cannot assess potential costs before submitting.

**Signature requirement**: The form requires a wet signature ("Print form and sign by hand"), which is a barrier to fully electronic submission. However, it does accept fax and email submission (the facility provides both fax and email addresses), so the form can be submitted remotely after printing and signing.

**No notarization requirement**: The form does not require notarization or witnesses.

**No expiration clause**: Unlike form-1 (which expires in 180 days), this patient access form contains no expiration language, which is appropriate ‚Äî a request for one's own records does not need an expiration.

**Representative access**: The form includes fields for "Representative Name," "Relation to Patient," and "Representative Signature" with a note to "Please include supporting documentation." This appropriately accommodates personal representatives without creating excessive barriers.

**Liability release language**: The form includes "The facility, its employees, officers and physicians are hereby released from any legal responsibility or liability for disclosure of the above information to the extent indicated and authorized herein." This language is unusual on a patient access form (more typical of third-party release forms) and may give patients pause, though it does not constitute an actionable barrier.

- **"Fees may be associated with this request"**: This vague statement gives patients no basis for estimating costs. - **Liability release on a patient access form**: The statement "The facility, its employees, officers and physicians are hereby released from any legal responsibility or liability for disclosure of the above information" is a legal release typically found on third-party authorization forms. Including it on a patient access form ‚Äî where the patient is simply exercising their HIPAA right ‚Äî is inappropriate and potentially coercive. It implies that accessing one's own records carries legal risk that the patient must assume.

- **Wet signature requirement despite 45 fillable fields**: The form has 45 interactive fillable fields but still requires patients to "Print form and sign by hand." This undermines the digital-first design. Under HIPAA, there is no requirement for a wet signature on a patient access request (as distinct from an authorization for third-party release, where a signature is required). Many organizations accept electronic or typed signatures for patient access requests.
