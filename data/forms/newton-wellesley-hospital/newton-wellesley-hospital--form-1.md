## 4. CONTENT DESIGN

**Structure**: The form is organized into five clearly lettered sections:
- **A. Patient information** ‚Äî name, DOB, MRN, address, phone
- **B. Permission to share** ‚Äî source facility, purpose checkboxes, recipient info, delivery method
- **C. Information to be released** ‚Äî 12 record type checkboxes, each requiring date specification
- **D. Sensitive information authorization** ‚Äî 7 categories with opt-in checkboxes
- **E. Understanding and agreement** ‚Äî 7 bullet points of disclosures, signature blocks

**Length**: 2 pages is proportionate for the content. The form packs substantial functionality without feeling cramped.

**Language clarity**: The form uses a mix of plain and legal language. Most instructions are clear: "Please print all information clearly in order to process your request in a timely manner." However, Section D contains dense regulatory language, particularly the 42 CFR Part 2 disclosure: "Federal rules prohibit any further disclosure of this information unless further disclosure is expressly permitted by written consent of the person to whom it pertains or as otherwise permitted by 42 CFR Part 2." This is legally required but not reader-friendly.

**Instruction at top**: The header prominently displays "Mail or Fax Release Form To:" with complete address and fax details, making submission mechanics immediately clear.

**Record type granularity**: Section C offers 12 specific record categories (clinic visit notes, discharge summary, lab reports, operative reports, pathology, radiation, radiology, photographs, billing records, medical record abstract, and "Other"). Each requires date specification, which helps patients be precise but also creates a burden ‚Äî patients who want "everything" must check all boxes and provide dates for each.

## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Patient access vs. third-party release**: This is a general authorization form that serves both purposes. The "Personal" purpose checkbox and the option to mail records "to the patient at the above address (section A)" accommodate patient self-requests, but the form is structurally oriented toward third-party release (Section B asks "Send records to" and requires recipient name/address unless the patient checks the self-mailing box).

**Delivery method options**: The form offers four delivery methods:
1. "Mass General Brigham Patient Gateway (if available)" ‚Äî the patient portal
2. "Secure Email" ‚Äî with email address field
3. "Fax"
4. "Paper Copy via Mail"

The inclusion of Patient Gateway and Secure Email as delivery options is a notable strength, offering electronic delivery paths.

**Scope options**: Patients can specify record types and dates. The date requirement for each record type category is granular but may frustrate patients who don't know exactly what records exist or their dates.

**Sensitive information handling**: Section D provides opt-in consent for seven categories of specially protected information: HIV test results, genetic screening, substance use disorder records (42 CFR Part 2), mental health treatment, social work communications, domestic violence counseling, and sexual assault counseling. Each requires an affirmative "Yes" checkbox. This is legally required and well-structured.

**Right of Access language**: The form does **not** explicitly reference HIPAA ¬ß 164.524 or the patient's Right of Access. It states that "This authorization is voluntary" and that treatment/payment won't be affected by refusal, but it doesn't frame the patient's request as a right.

**EHI Export**: No mention of electronic health information export, bulk data, or USCDI.

**Multilingual support**: Per the notes, the MGB page offers the form in English, Spanish, and Portuguese ‚Äî a meaningful accessibility feature.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

**Fee disclosure**: The form notes "*Copying fees may apply" next to the Insurance, Legal, and Other purpose checkboxes. Notably, "Personal" and "Medical Care" purposes are **not** asterisked, implying no copying fees for patient access requests ‚Äî which aligns with HIPAA's reasonable cost-based fee limitation for patient access.

**Expiration**: "This authorization will automatically expire 6 months from the date signed unless otherwise specified: __________." Six months is a standard and reasonable expiration window, and the ability to specify an alternative timeframe is a positive feature.

**Revocation**: Patients "may cancel this authorization at any time by submitting a written request to the Department or Office where I originally submitted it," with standard exceptions for actions already taken. This is compliant.

**No notarization required**: The form requires only a patient signature (or legal representative signature for minors/incapacitated patients).

**Submission methods**: Mail and fax are accepted; there is no indication of in-person-only submission requirements. The header instructions are "Mail or Fax Release Form To:" ‚Äî no electronic submission option for the form itself is mentioned (though records can be delivered electronically).

**Outside records disclaimer**: Section E includes: "I understand that if Mass General Brigham maintains any of my records from outside providers, these will not be released unless I specifically ask for them under 'Other' in section C." This is worth noting ‚Äî patients must explicitly request outside provider records that MGB holds, which could result in incomplete releases if patients aren't aware of this limitation.

**Pick-up identification**: The "For Internal Use Only" section at the bottom notes pick-up identification options (License, State ID, Passport, Other Photo ID), suggesting in-person pick-up is available as an additional delivery method.
