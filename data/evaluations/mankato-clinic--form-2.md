## 4. CONTENT DESIGN

**Structure and organization**: The form follows a logical top-to-bottom flow: Patient identification ‚Üí Source of records ‚Üí Destination for records ‚Üí Information to be disclosed ‚Üí Reason for release ‚Üí Legal/revocation language ‚Üí Signature. The second page provides clear section-by-section instructions. This is well-organized.

**Section labels**: This older version uses more formal, less patient-friendly labels compared to form-1. Where form-1 says "Release my medical records from" and "Share my medical records with," this version uses "Health Care Facility / Provider" and "Requestor" ‚Äî terms that are less intuitive for patients.

**Contact information**: The header provides contact details for three ROI locations (Main, Wick/CHC, North) with separate phone and fax numbers for each, plus an email address (ROI@mankato-clinic.com). This is helpful but slightly cluttered.

**Checkbox options for record types**: The form offers 11 checkbox options for record types: Past 2 years of records, Clinic/Hospital records, X-ray reports, X-Ray film/CD/other, Lab/Pathology Reports, Immunizations, HIV/AIDS records, Financial/Billing, Mental health notes, Last colonoscopy/mammogram/Pap/eye exam, and Communication (Verbal/Written). This gives reasonable specificity.

**Reason options**: Five pre-set reasons (Legal, Insurance, Personal, Continuation of Medical Care, Transferring care) plus "Other" and a unique "No records needed at this time; keep on file" option.

**Instruction page**: Page 2 provides field-by-field guidance, which is genuinely helpful. It includes practical details like "We prioritize requests by appointment date" and "Please expect at least 7-10 business days from receipt of your request for processing."

**Language clarity**: The legal/revocation section is reasonably clear, though it uses longer sentences that could be simplified. The statement "I understand that Mankato Clinic will not condition my treatment on whether I sign this authorization form" properly addresses unconditional treatment, with appropriate exceptions noted for research and fitness-for-work exams.

**Layout**: The form is moderately dense on page 1 but not cramped. Visual hierarchy is maintained through bold section headers and boxed groupings.

## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Patient access vs. third-party release**: This form does **not** differentiate between a patient requesting their own records and a third-party authorization. The bidirectional source/destination model means a patient requesting their own records would need to list themselves as the "Requestor," which is workable but not intuitive. There is no mention of HIPAA's Right of Access, patient rights language, or any indication that patients have a legal right to their records.

**Scope control**: Patients can specify diagnosis/treatment and date ranges via a free-text field ("Records concerning: ___ Specific Diagnosis or Treatment and Specific Dates of Service") and can select specific record types via checkboxes. This provides adequate scope control.

**Delivery format options**: The form offers "COPIES: GIVEN / MAILED / FAXED ON: ___ / CALL WHEN READY / PICK UP" ‚Äî but no electronic delivery option (no email, no portal, no download). The instructions page explicitly states: "Medical records are not able to be emailed." This limits patients to physical pickup, mail, or fax.

**Turnaround time**: The instructions state "at least 7-10 business days," which is within the HIPAA 30-day requirement but not particularly fast.

**Fees**: The authorization section states "I understand there may be a charge associated with the Release of Information services rendered" ‚Äî but does not disclose what those charges are. Under HIPAA's Right of Access, fees for patient-access requests are limited to a reasonable, cost-based fee, but patients reading this form have no idea what to expect.

**Communication option**: The form includes a "Communication (check one / both) Verbal / Written" option, which appears to allow patients to authorize another person to receive verbal or written communication about their records. This is a useful feature.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

**Authorization expiration**: "This authorization will be in effect for 12 months from the date signed unless cancelled by me in writing." A 12-month expiration is standard and reasonable.

**Treatment conditioning**: The form correctly states treatment will not be conditioned on signing, with appropriate exceptions.

**Re-disclosure warning**: "I understand that once information is released pursuant to this authorization, Mankato Clinic cannot prevent the re-disclosure of the information to another third party." This is a standard and accurate HIPAA disclosure.

**Witness requirement**: The form includes a "Witnessed by:" line. While not labeled as mandatory, the presence of a witness line on an ROI form is unusual and could be perceived as an additional barrier. HIPAA does not require witnessed authorization for patient access requests.

**No re-release policy**: The instructions state "Mankato Clinic does not re-release medical records from other facilities." This is a policy disclosure, not a HIPAA barrier, but it's relevant for patients who received care across multiple facilities.

**Photo ID for pickup**: "Please bring a photo ID with you in order to pick up medical records." This is a reasonable identity verification measure.

**Legal representative documentation**: "Spouses or parents of children 18 and older are not able to sign for patient unless they are a legal representative of the patient and can provide appropriate documentation." This is accurate and appropriately protective.
