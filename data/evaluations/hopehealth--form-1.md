## 1. DOCUMENT IDENTIFICATION

This document is **not** a Release of Information (ROI) or Authorization form. It is HopeHealth's **Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP)** ‚Äî a HIPAA-mandated document describing how the organization may use and disclose patient medical information. The document title is "NOTICE OF PRIVACY PRACTICES" and it opens with: *"This notice describes how medical information about you may be used and disclosed and how you can get access to this information."*

It was downloaded from `https://www.hope-health.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Notice-of-Privacy-Practices_26.pdf`, linked on HopeHealth's website. It took effect September 23, 2013 and was amended February 16, 2026.

This is one of two documents retrieved for HopeHealth. ## 4. CONTENT DESIGN

The NPP covers five numbered sections: (1) Our Pledge Regarding Medical Information, (2) Our Legal Duty, (3) Use and Disclosure of Your Medical Information, and (4) Your Individual Rights, and (5) Privacy Correspondence.

**Length vs. readability**: Compressing this volume of content onto a single page is a significant design problem. Standard NPP documents of similar scope typically run 4‚Äì8 pages. The 12,528 characters of text on one page means extremely dense formatting with minimal whitespace and very small font sizes.

**Language**: The document mixes plain-language explanations with legal terminology. The treatment/payment/operations section uses helpful concrete examples (e.g., *"You are in the hospital with a broken leg. You also have diabetes."*), which is a positive practice. However, the legal sections (law enforcement, specialized government functions, court orders) are dense and would challenge many readers.

**Organization**: The five-section structure is logical, progressing from organizational pledge ‚Üí legal obligations ‚Üí uses/disclosures ‚Üí patient rights ‚Üí contact information. Subsections within Section 3 are labeled by topic (Treatment, Payment, Health Care Operations, etc.), which aids navigation.

**Completeness**: The NPP covers standard categories including treatment, payment, operations, fundraising, research, law enforcement, public health, and disaster relief. It addresses substance use disorder (SUD) records and psychotherapy notes. The fundraising section includes opt-out information with a phone number: *"(888) 841-5855 and ask to speak to the Director of Corporate Compliance."*

## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

As an NPP, this document serves an informational purpose rather than a transactional one. Evaluating it from the perspective of a patient trying to access their own records:

**Right of Access language**: Section 4 lists six patient rights, beginning with: *"Look at or get copies of your medical information. You may request that we provide copies in a format other than photocopies. We will use the format you request unless it is not practical for us to do so."* This references the right to choose format, which aligns with HIPAA ¬ß 164.524. However, it does not cite the regulation by number, does not mention fees, does not mention the 30-day response timeline, and does not reference electronic copies or patient portal access.

**Actionability gap**: The NPP tells patients they have the right to access records but provides no practical mechanism to exercise that right. It says *"You must make your request in writing"* and *"You may get the form to request access by using the contact information listed at the end of this notice"* ‚Äî but the contact section (Section 5) provides only the Privacy Officer's phone number, email, and mailing address. There is no link to the HealthMark portal, no mention of the online request process, and no downloadable authorization form referenced.

**No mention of electronic delivery, patient portal, or EHI export.** The only format-related language is the general statement about requesting copies "in a format other than photocopies."

## 6. COMPLIANCE

This is an NPP rather than an authorization form, so standard ROI compliance concerns (notarization, in-person requirements, excessive fees, expiration periods) do not directly apply. However, several aspects of the NPP merit comment:

- **No fee disclosure**: The document does not disclose what fees, if any, HopeHealth charges for copies of medical records. HIPAA permits reasonable, cost-based fees for patient access requests, and best practice is to disclose these in the NPP or related materials.
- **No timeline disclosure**: The NPP does not mention the 30-day (extendable to 60-day) response window required by HIPAA for access requests.
- **Vague request process**: Telling patients to make requests "in writing" and referencing a form available via "the contact information listed at the end" ‚Äî when the contact section doesn't include a form or portal link ‚Äî creates a dead end.
