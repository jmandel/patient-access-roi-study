## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Strengths**: The form clearly explains the patient's right to request amendment and provides a step-by-step walkthrough of what happens after submission. The 60-day timeline is explicitly stated, along with the possibility of a 30-day extension. The form also tells patients they can file complaints with HHS and provides a direct URL (`http://www.hhs.gov/ocr/privacy/index.html`). Contact information for the Corporate Compliance Director is provided with a direct phone extension.

**Concerns**: 
- The form asks for "Last Four Digits of SSN," which, while less than a full SSN, is unnecessary for an amendment request when MRN and Date of Birth are already collected. This creates a minor privacy risk for a document that must be printed and physically transported.
- Writing space is limited to 7 lines for what could be a complex medical record dispute. While the form acknowledges this with "use a separate sheet of paper," the instruction "DO NOT write on the back of this form" is unnecessarily restrictive for a printed form.
- There are only three delivery methods (mail, fax, in-person) ‚Äî no email or online submission option. Given that ACP has a Swellbox-based electronic records request wizard, the absence of an electronic pathway for amendment requests is a missed opportunity.
- The form requires "one form for each amendment request," which could be burdensome for patients with multiple disputed entries.

**Language**: English only. Unlike the minor release form (forms 5‚Äì6 which have Spanish versions), no Spanish-language version of the amendment request form was found among the downloaded forms.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

The form aligns with HIPAA's amendment provisions (45 CFR ¬ß 164.526) in several respects:
- It acknowledges the patient's right to request amendment.
- It states the 60-day response deadline with a permissible 30-day extension.
- It notes the right to submit a statement of disagreement upon denial.
- It informs patients of their right to file complaints with HHS.

**ID verification**: The office-use section includes a field for "Form of ID presented for verification" with options for Driver's License, Government ID, or Other. While identity verification is reasonable, requiring in-person ID presentation could create a barrier for patients who mail or fax the form. The form doesn't clarify how identity is verified for mailed/faxed submissions.

**No fee mentioned**: The form does not mention any fee for processing an amendment request, which is correct ‚Äî HIPAA does not permit fees for amendment requests.
