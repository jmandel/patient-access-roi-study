## 6. COMPLIANCE

**Authorization expiration:** The form includes a reasonable expiration mechanism: "Fill in the Date or the Event but not both. If Date or Event not filled out, authorization will expire 1 year after date of signing." A 1-year default is standard.

**Voluntary signing:** Item 1 states "I may refuse to sign this Authorization and that it is strictly voluntary." Item 2 adds "My treatment, payment, enrollment or eligibility for benefits will not be conditioned on signing this Authorization." Both are required HIPAA elements and correctly stated.

**Revocation rights:** Item 3 provides a specific address for written revocation. This is appropriate.

**Re-disclosure warning:** Item 4 covers the re-disclosure risk, including a mention that "State law limiting re-disclosure of specifically protected categories may continue to apply to any recipient." This is a more nuanced statement than some forms provide.

**ID requirement:** The instructions (Section C, item 3) state: "Please provide a copy of the patient's ID or the legal paperwork mentioned above, along with this request." Requiring ID for patient access requests is standard practice, though the phrasing could be read as requiring ID even for third-party releases that are authorized by the patient's signature.

**Fee structure:** The vague "fees may be based on the number of pages" language, combined with "The fee schedule will be provided on request," does not disclose specific amounts. For patient access requests under HIPAA, fees are limited to reasonable cost-based fees. The lack of specific disclosure isn't a violation but is less transparent than ideal.
