## 4. CONTENT DESIGN

**Length and proportion:** The form fits on a single page, which is efficient. However, fitting all content ‚Äî patient demographics, authorization direction, record type selection, sensitive-information authorization, date ranges, disclosure purpose statement, redisclosure prohibition notice, and signature blocks ‚Äî onto one page likely results in a cramped layout given the 9 embedded images and 7 fonts.

**Introductory language:** The form opens with a clear, plain-language instruction: "PLEASE READ: You should only fill out this form if you want to have medical or dental information sent to Anchorage Neighborhood Health Center (ANHC), or if you want information from your ANHC records provided to another source." This effectively frames the form's two purposes.

**Information type selection:** The form provides checkboxes for six specific record types (History and Physical, Progress Notes, Lab Reports, X-Ray Reports, Immunization Records, Medication List) plus an "Other" free-text field. This gives patients reasonable granularity in scoping their request.

**Sensitive information:** A separate section requires additional explicit authorization for three categories: Substance Use Disorder, Mental Health, and HIV-Related Information. These are appropriately segregated per federal and state requirements (42 CFR Part 2 for SUD).

**Date range options:** Two pre-set options ‚Äî "Records from the past 12 months" and "Records from most recent 3 visits" ‚Äî plus a free-text "Notes" field. The "past 12 months" option appears pre-checked with an "X" in the extracted text. This is helpful but limited; there's no option for "all records" or a custom date range field (only the notes line).

**Language:** The disclosure/purpose section uses moderately dense but not impenetrable legal language. Key passages are reasonably clear: "I understand that authorizing the disclosure of this health information is voluntary" and "my treatment, health care, and payment for my heath care will not be affected if I do not sign this form."

**Demographics collected:** Patient Name, DOB, Last Four Digits of SS#, Phone, Evening Phone, Address/City/State/Zip. Requesting the last four SSN digits is notable ‚Äî while common in healthcare, it is not required for a records request and adds friction and data exposure risk.

## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

**Patient access vs. third-party release:** The form is a dual-purpose document covering both patient-initiated record requests and third-party releases. The "Obtain" vs. "Release" checkbox structure implies that when a patient wants their own records, they would check "Release Information to" and list themselves (or their personal address) as the recipient. This is functional but not optimized for patient self-service ‚Äî there is no dedicated "I want my own records" pathway or simplified patient access request form.

**Right of Access reference:** The form explicitly cites HIPAA: "*Note: Per HIPAA guidelines, patients are NOT required to give a purpose for their use of health information Individuals' Right under HIPAA to Access their Health Information 45 CFR ¬ß 164.524." This is a strong patient-rights statement, directly informing patients they don't need to justify their request. However, the citation formatting is slightly garbled ‚Äî there appears to be a missing period or line break between "health information" and "Individuals' Right."

**Format/delivery options:** The form provides no options for electronic delivery. There is a fax number field for the recipient, suggesting fax is a supported delivery method for third-party releases, but no mention of email, patient portal download, CD/USB, or other electronic formats.

**Fee disclosure:** The form states: "In order to offset the cost of maintaining and producing copies of the medical record, ANHC does charge for copies. There is no charge for medical records if copies are sent to other health care facilities for ongoing care or follow-up treatment." This acknowledges fees exist but does not specify the amount. Notably, it only mentions free copies for facility-to-facility transfers, not for patient self-access ‚Äî under HIPAA's Right of Access, fees for patient-requested copies must be limited to a reasonable, cost-based fee.

**Expiration:** The authorization expires "one (1) year after I have signed this form," which is reasonable and clearly stated.

**Revocation:** Clearly stated ‚Äî the patient may "revoke this authorization at any time by notifying Anchorage Neighborhood Health Center in writing."

## 6. COMPLIANCE

**42 CFR Part 2 (SUD):** The form includes a separate authorization section for Substance Use Disorder information and a full "PROHIBITION OF REDISCLOSURE" notice for 42 CFR Part 2 protected information. This is appropriately handled.

**Fee structure concern:** The fee language draws a distinction between copies sent to healthcare facilities (free) and implicitly all other copies (charged). Under 45 CFR ¬ß 164.524(c)(4), patients requesting their own records may only be charged a reasonable, cost-based fee. The form's language could be read to suggest patients requesting their own records face charges, while the free-copy exemption only applies to facility transfers. This could deter patients from exercising their Right of Access.

**SSN collection:** Requesting the "Last Four Digits of SS#" is not a HIPAA requirement for processing a records request. While common as an identity verification aid, it adds unnecessary data collection risk and could be a barrier for patients uncomfortable sharing this information.

**Identity verification:** The form includes an "ID VERIFIED BY ANHC EMPLOYEE" field in the office-use section, which is appropriate. It asks for the employee name, verification method, and initials ‚Äî suggesting a structured in-person or verifiable process.

**Representative access:** The form notes "Parent/Guardian, or Representative (legal documents required)" ‚Äî requiring legal documentation for representatives is appropriate and standard.
