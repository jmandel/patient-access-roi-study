## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

This form has notable patient-centeredness gaps:

- **Third-party framing**: The title ("Authorization to Release Medical Records to Third Party") signals this is designed for disclosures to others, not for a patient requesting their own records. A patient seeking their own EHI would need to navigate a form that conceptually treats them as authorizing a release *to* someone else.
- **"Patient's Request" is just one of eight purposes**: It is listed alongside Insurance, Legal, Military, etc. ‚Äî there is no dedicated patient-access pathway or simplified form for this common use case.
- **SSN collection**: The form requests "Last 4 Digits of SSN" for patient identification. While partial SSN is less risky than full SSN, HIPAA does not require SSN for record requests, and collecting it creates unnecessary data exposure risk. Many patients may be uncomfortable providing this.
- **No submission instructions**: The form does not tell the patient where to send the completed form ‚Äî no fax number, no mailing address, no email address, no portal link. The revocation section references notifying the "Privacy Officer" at a blank address field, but there is no guidance for initial form submission.
- **No fee disclosure**: The form does not mention any fees that may be charged for record copies. Under HIPAA, providers may charge a reasonable, cost-based fee, but patients should be informed upfront.
- **No timeline expectations**: There is no mention of how long the provider will take to process the request (HIPAA allows up to 30 days with a possible 30-day extension).
- **Delivery method options are limited**: Only "E-Delivery" and "Mail" are offered as delivery methods. There is no option for in-person pickup, patient portal delivery, or specification of electronic format preferences.
- **"Verbal Communication Only" as a record type**: This option in the information request section is unusual and unclear ‚Äî it could mean the patient only wants a verbal summary rather than written records, which could be used to deny written copies.
- **No multilingual support**: The form is English-only.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

- **Authorization duration**: One year, consistent with HIPAA norms. The "or the date on which Patient no longer receives services from Provider, whichever is later" extension is a standard provision.
- **Revocation rights**: Properly disclosed ‚Äî patient can revoke in writing, and revocation is not retroactive. However, the address for revocation is left blank (a fill-in field), which is problematic if the patient doesn't know where to send revocation.
- **Voluntary statement**: Correctly states that signing is optional and treatment/payment will not be conditioned on signing.
- **Re-disclosure warning**: Present and appropriately stated.
- **Sensitive information**: Properly separates HIV/AIDS, substance use, genetic testing, and mental health/developmental disabilities as requiring specific authorization, consistent with federal and many state laws.
- **No explicit HIPAA citation**: The form references "federal HIPAA privacy rules" but does not cite 45 CFR ¬ß 164.508 or other specific regulatory provisions.
- **Third-party release framing vs. patient access**: This form is structured as a ¬ß 164.508 authorization for third-party disclosure, not as a ¬ß 164.524 patient access request. While it can technically be used for patient access (via the "Patient's Request" checkbox), conflating these two distinct HIPAA rights in a single form may create confusion. Patient access requests under ¬ß 164.524 have different rules (lower fee caps, shorter timelines, fewer permissible denials) than third-party authorizations under ¬ß 164.508.
