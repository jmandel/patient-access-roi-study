## 4. CONTENT DESIGN

**Structure**: The instructions follow the numbered sections of form-3, making it straightforward to cross-reference. Six numbered steps walk through patient identification, specifying the releasing entity, delivery method, record types, substance use/HIV information, and signature requirements.

**Clarity**: The language is generally accessible and uses a helpful conversational tone. Examples are specific and concrete — e.g., "ex. North Shore University Hospital, or Imaging at Smithtown" and "Dr. Jane Jones at 300 Northwell Drive." The instructions explain practical implications: "If only one doctor is listed, then Northwell Health will only release that specific location information."

**Helpful guidance**: The document provides genuinely useful advice about the "Medical Record Abstract" option, explaining what it includes: "Provider/Clinician Documentation, Procedure Reports, Laboratory, Pathology, and Radiology Reports" for doctor's offices, and a slightly different set for hospitals. This helps patients make informed choices about what to request.

**Length**: At 2 pages for 6 instructional sections, the length is proportionate. The text is not cramped or overwhelming.

**Potential confusion**: Step 2 says "Select an option and enter the complete name of person/physician/facility/company to receive the information (the destination of the records)." This mirrors the form's structure but the phrase "Select an option" is unclear without the form in hand since the instructions don't reproduce the checkbox choices ("To me" / "To Another Person or Entity") visible in form-3.

## 5. PATIENT-CENTEREDNESS

As a companion to the patient access form (form-3), this document is specifically oriented toward patient self-requests. However, it could do more to emphasize patient rights. Unlike form-3 itself, these instructions make **no mention of HIPAA, the right of access, or 45 CFR 164.524**. A brief statement reminding patients of their legal right to access their records would have been appropriate here.

The instructions do address delivery options (Step 3), noting mail, electronic mail, fax, and pickup. They note that "If this information is not included, records will be sent via regular mail" — a useful default disclosure.

The document explains the substance use disorder and HIV/AIDS consent requirements (Step 5), including purpose and expiration date requirements, which helps patients understand why these additional fields exist.

Notably absent: any mention of expected turnaround times, fees, or how to submit the completed form (mail, fax, in-person, patient portal). A patient reading these instructions knows *how to fill out the form* but not *what to do with it once completed*.

## 6. COMPLIANCE

This is an instruction document, not a form, so it does not directly impose compliance requirements. However, several compliance-relevant observations emerge:

- **Separate request requirement**: Step 1 states "There should only be one patient listed on the form," and Step 2 states "If records are needed by more than one person/facility, a separate request is required." While the single-patient rule is standard, requiring a separate request per receiving entity could create burden for patients who need records sent to multiple providers.

- **Expiration requirement**: Step 5 instructs patients that "The expiration date, event, or condition upon which the form will expire must be a fixed period of time (e.g., 1 Year, 30 days post discharge)." This is appropriate for substance use disorder records under 42 CFR Part 2 but this section applies only to SUD/HIV records, not the general request.

- **No fee disclosure**: The instructions do not mention any fees associated with record access, which leaves patients unable to anticipate costs.

## 7. NOTABLE OBSERVATIONS

### ✨ BRIGHT SPOTS

- **Dedicated patient access instructions**: Northwell maintains a separate instruction document specifically for patient self-access requests, distinct from the third-party release instructions (form-2). This two-form, two-instructions-document approach demonstrates organizational investment in the patient access workflow.

- **Medical Record Abstract explanation**: The detailed breakdown of what a "Medical Record Abstract" contains for doctor's offices versus hospitals is genuinely helpful. Patients are often unsure what to request, and explaining that the abstract covers "Provider/Clinician Documentation, Procedure Reports, Laboratory, Pathology, and Radiology Reports" over "the past 1-2 years" provides actionable guidance.

- **Concrete examples throughout**: The use of sample names ("Jane Patient"), addresses, and facility examples ("North Shore University Hospital, or Imaging at Smithtown") makes the instructions tangible rather than abstract.

### ⚠️ AMBIGUITIES

- **No submission instructions**: The document explains how to complete the form but never states how to submit it — no mailing address, fax number, patient portal mention, or email address for submission. A patient could complete the form perfectly and still not know where to send it.

- **"Select an option" in Step 2** references form-3's "To me" / "To Another Person or Entity" checkboxes, but without reproducing those options, the instruction is opaque if read without the form in hand.

### ❌ ERRORS

- **Footer title truncation/inconsistency**: The footer reads "Northwell Health – Request for Access to Health Information Management by the Patient or Personal Representative" — note the word "Management" inserted, making it read as though the patient is requesting access to "Health Information Management" rather than to "Health Information." The form-3 footer (VD087) does not contain this word. This appears to be a titling error in the instructions document.
